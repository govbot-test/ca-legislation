LEGISLATIVE COUNSEL'S DIGESTSR 118, as introduced, Niello. Digest KeyBill TextWHEREAS, Prostate cancer is the most commonly diagnosed nonskin cancer and the second leading cause of cancer-related deaths among men in the United States; andWHEREAS, Approximately 333,830 men in the United States will be diagnosed with prostate cancer in 2026, and approximately 36,320 men will die from the disease, equating to one death every 15 minutes; andWHEREAS, More than 3,300,000 men in the United States are currently living with prostate cancer; andWHEREAS, One in 8 men will be diagnosed with prostate cancer during their lifetime, and 1 in 41 will die from the disease; andWHEREAS, African American men face disproportionately higher risks, with approximately one in six diagnosed and mortality rates nearly twice that of other populations; andWHEREAS, The risk of prostate cancer increases significantly after 50 years of age and is higher among individuals with a family history of the disease; andWHEREAS, Prostate cancer often presents no noticeable symptoms in its early stages, making early detection through appropriate screening critically important; andWHEREAS, Screening methods, including prostate-specific antigen blood tests and digital rectal examinations, may detect prostate cancer at an early and more treatable stage; andWHEREAS, When detected early, the survival rate for localized and regional prostate cancer approaches nearly 100 percent, while survival rates drop significantly once the cancer has metastasized; andWHEREAS, Recent trends indicate that prostate cancer screening and routine testing may decline or be discontinued for some patients without adequate consultation or understanding of the associated risks; andWHEREAS, It is essential that patients are aware of any changes to recommended testing practices and are empowered to make informed decisions about their health; andWHEREAS, Individuals are strongly encouraged to speak with their physicians to understand their personal risk factors, the benefits and risks of screening, and whether continued testing is appropriate for them; andWHEREAS, Increasing public awareness, education, and proactive communication between patients and health care providers can save lives and protect families; now, therefore, be itResolved by the Senate of the State of California, That the Senate hereby designates September 2026 as Prostate Cancer Awareness Month in California; and be it furtherResolved, That the Senate encourages all individuals, particularly those at higher risk, to engage in informed discussions with their health care providers regarding prostate cancer screening and early detection; and be it furtherResolved, That the Senate urges increased awareness of the importance of continued vigilance in prostate cancer testing; and be it furtherResolved, That the Secretary of the Senate transmit copies of this resolution to the author for appropriate distribution.
